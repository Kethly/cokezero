### What the data suggests
- Consumers are increasingly choosing “zero sugar” or “diet” versions. :contentReference[oaicite:22]{index=22}
- Despite growth in these categories, the total “healthy drink” shift is not automatic — many consumers still opt for traditional sodas, and substitution is incomplete.
- Market signals show opportunities for brands, but also concerns for public health: growth in zero-sugar options doesn’t necessarily equal less overall soda consumption or improved health outcomes.

### Health & policy implications
- Rapid growth of zero-sugar products means public health messaging must adapt. Just because a beverage is labelled “zero” doesn’t guarantee it is benign.
- Labeling policies matter: experiments show warning or content labels can modestly reduce soda purchasing. For instance, in Mexico, self-reported reductions in soda purchases followed introduction of warning-labels. :contentReference[oaicite:23]{index=23}
- Growth in market size may reflect both consumer demand and reformulation by manufacturers — but the underlying issue of beverage-overconsumption remains.

Conclusion

The period from 2020 to 2025 marks a phase of transition in the soda world. Zero-sugar and diet categories are growing rapidly, but the challenge for public health remains: shifting long-entrenched drinking habits, addressing perceptions of “healthier soda”, and ensuring growth of alternatives leads to real health benefits—not just marketing wins.
