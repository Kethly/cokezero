Opinion: Are “zero” labels messing with our brains?

When a can says “Zero Sugar,” do we assume health? That’s the problem.

By Opinion Editor Opinion October 29, 2025

“Zero sugar” claims can shape perception beyond nutrition. Experiments show sugar‑free labels raise willingness‑to‑pay because people infer “healthier,” even when taste expectations change. Warning‑label experiments, meanwhile, can reduce perceived healthfulness and purchase intent of sugary drinks.

Labels alter beliefs: sugar‑free tags can boost willingness‑to‑pay; warning labels can reduce purchase intent in controlled studies.

Why it matters

If a label convinces us a drink is “guilt‑free,” we might drink more of it or relax elsewhere (bigger meals, less movement). That’s a psychology issue, not a nutrition fact.
