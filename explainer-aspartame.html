Explainer: What is Aspartame, actually?

A quick guide to the sugar substitute found in many diet sodas.

Health Updated Oct 29, 2025 • Washington, D.C.

Short version: Aspartame is a low-calorie sweetener ~200× sweeter than sugar. It’s widely used and heavily studied. In July 2023, WHO’s cancer agency IARC labeled it “possibly carcinogenic to humans” (Group 2B), while the WHO/FAO committee JECFA reaffirmed the acceptable daily intake (ADI) of 0–40 mg/kg/day. The U.S. FDA says current evidence supports safety at approved levels.

Where it’s used

Diet/“zero” sodas, sugar-free gum, tabletop sweeteners, and various low-calorie foods.

What regulators say

  • IARC (WHO): Group 2B “possibly carcinogenic,” based on limited human and animal evidence (2023).
  • JECFA (WHO/FAO): Kept ADI at 40 mg/kg/day in 2023.
  • FDA (US): Disagrees with IARC’s hazard classification; maintains safety within current uses (updated 2025).
  • EFSA (EU): Comprehensive 2013 review: safe at current exposure; ongoing re-evaluations.

Special case: PKU

People with phenylketonuria (PKU) must avoid aspartame because one of its components, phenylalanine, cannot be properly metabolized.

Bottom line

For most people, moderate intake is considered safe by major food-safety bodies. The IARC note signals a potential hazard; it is not a direct measure of real-world risk. As with any sweet drink, context and overall diet matter.
