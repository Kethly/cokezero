New Study Finds That a Single Diet Cola Is Basically a Pack of Cigarettes Wearing a Fruit Hat

Scientists call it “deeply concerning.” Beverage companies call it “deeply inconvenient.”

By Evelyn Jones Washington, D.C. October 29, 2025 Health & Science
Share Bookmark Print

WASHINGTON, D.C. — In a shocking revelation that scientists are calling “deeply concerning” and beverage companies are calling “deeply inconvenient,” a new study from the NIH has found that drinking just one Diet Cola is roughly equivalent to smoking an entire pack of cigarettes — if those cigarettes were carbonated, calorie-free, and slightly cherry-flavored.

Researchers say the findings came after observing lab rats that consumed a Diet Cola daily for three weeks. “By the end, the rats had tiny Fitbits, deep existential dread, and a sudden urge to debate strangers about macros,” said lead scientist Dr. Linda Carte. “In short, they had turned into humans.”

Health officials warn that the combination of aspartame, artificial coloring, and “that weird cold burn when it hits the throat” triggers a physiological response similar to chain-smoking while riding a Peloton. “You might feel healthier because there are zero calories,” said Dr. Carte, “but so does a ghost.”

Diet Cola defenders argue that the research is exaggerated. “Correlation isn’t causation,” said beverage industry spokesperson Chad Firton, sipping from a suspiciously branded cup. “If Diet Cola were really that bad, would it be available in every gas station, middle school vending machine, and hospital cafeteria? Checkmate, science.”

Still, the public reaction has been swift. Major grocery chains have already moved Diet Cola behind the counter next to the Marlboros. Some cities are considering “Soda-Free Zones” where people can enjoy public spaces without the faint hiss of carbonation or the unmistakable sound of a plastic cap twisting off.

“WARNING: Consuming Diet Cola may cause delusions of hydration, temporary feelings of productivity, and eventual regret.”

The FDA is reportedly exploring mandatory health warnings, including one prototype label that reads the above.

Meanwhile, former soda enthusiasts are turning to safer alternatives like black coffee, kombucha, or staring into the middle distance until the craving passes.

At press time, researchers confirmed that people who drink Diet Cola through a metal straw are 12% more likely to lecture strangers about “gut health” — while secretly Googling whether sparkling water counts as hydration.

Please drink Diet Cola and Coke Zero with caution.
